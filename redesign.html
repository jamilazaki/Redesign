Special Forces 805

By Jamila Zaki

scroll ↓

Special Forces 805 is an anti-fascist punk band originating from San Luis Obispo, Calif. It consists of singer Jose "Luis" Lange, guitarist Reily "Big Rye" Lowry, bassist Tyler Sheldon and drummer Micah Betros. They are Cal Poly students who use music to share their personal struggles and advocate on social issues.

They cover topics regarding disability, immigration and politics. A main source of inspiration for their music comes from Lange's experience with disability. "When I was eight months old I had a stroke and that affected the left side of my body," Lange said. "I have pretty good dexterity on the right side, but the left side [not so much]."

I'm Retarded, You're Fucking Stupid Special Forces 805

In their song "Special Forces" from their first album, "I'm Retarded, You're Fucking Stupid," he illustrates a part of his childhood as going to physical therapy, wearing an ankle brace to walk and feeling "othered."

"Growing up different than the other boys.

Physical therapy instead of playing with toys."

— Jose "Luis" Lange, Special Forces 805

To give back to their community, they collaborate with other bands to perform at local venues and host benefit shows. On April 24, they hosted a benefit show for Lumina Alliance, a local nonprofit that provides support and resources for survivors of sexual and intimate partner violence.

They have also donated to organizations like Disabled American Veterans, 805UndocuFund, Prosthetics for Palestine and more. Since starting in February 2025, Special Forces 805 has grown a massive amount of support online.

Milo James, a student at Paso Robles High School, loves how the local punk scene is interconnected and looks out for its own. "I'm disabled, and seeing representation of my struggles in the music I love is so beautiful and important to me," he said. "Unapologetic, loud, and in your face-we aren't going anywhere." Photo courtesy of Jamila Zaki

Will Murphy, lead singer of Idle Worship and a contestant in the MPU x FIJI Battle of the Bands, has been part of the scene for the past seven years. "It turned into a safe haven for everyone who wanted to party or dance but not have to conform to the ultra-white, conservative, frat drinking culture," he said. Special Forces 805's music resonates with many because of its raw honesty and relatability.

Since the beginning of humankind, music has been used to foster community and connection. Music influences culture, emotions and outlook on life. According to Save the Music, Lia Peralta writes, "We connect with others via music, especially those who produce or perform it — we recite their lyrics, dance to their melodies, and form a sense of connection through their self-expression."

"I think that a lot of people can relate to the music because it's so honest and real," Lowry said. "It's not like we're trying to be something we're not. We're just being ourselves, and I think that people can really connect with that."

Oliver Taylor, a student at Paso Robles High School and a fan within the scene, connects to what the band sings. "A lot of people would tell me that you're faking this, you're faking that, your problems don't actually mean that much because there's nothing physically or visibly wrong with me, so I felt very ashamed and as if my experiences weren't valid," he said. "Since I discovered Special Forces, it has been an outlet for me and a way to embrace my disability."
